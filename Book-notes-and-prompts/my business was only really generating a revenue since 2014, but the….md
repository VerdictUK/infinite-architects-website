Houston, We Have A Problem. [Ch.1].  
  
  
  
It’s July 2015, and I’m in Houston, Texas, after travelling from London. I was asked to be a keynote speaker at a conference I was invited to by Vicky Hamilton, Guns N’ Roses’ former manager. I was seated on the panel with Drake’s associate to my right, and Paul McCartney’s sister Ruth to my left, facing an audience of over 500 people. Whilst surveying the attentive assembly around us, a line of 40 artists are eagerly queued behind a microphone, waiting to ask our expert opinion about ways in which they could improve their own music careers.   
  
  
  
An individual from this group of aspiring artists asked “How would we go about branding ourselves better?” I looked at Ruth and Anthony to my side, they knew I was the one to answer this one. I began to speak...  
  
  
  
After my first talk on “Making Music Viral” and following my “Major Marketing” panel, I bumped into this guy in his mid-forties, average height and authoritative, but casually dressed, wearing a baseball cap and a black t-shirt. He didn’t appear to be a musician, hence my intrigue - who was this guy and what was he doing here? I asked “What’s your name?”, and following humbly in a strong Nashville accent he replied “Hey, I’m Rick”, handing me his business card with habitual dexterity and a pleasant smile on his face - he knew who I was. I turned the card over in my hands and acknowledged he was one of the main speakers at the event - the man behind Taylor Swift’s rise to international stardom. He stood in on our event with clear vision regarding the music industry. This guy was networking with me.   
  
  
  
I was engaged in three conferences over the busy weekend, with crowds of up to a thousand people. During the third event, industry heavyweights including Rick Barker, Ruth McCartney, Vicky Hamilton, Barry Coffing, A.Cole and myself, among others, continued to chat, arranging bilateral meetings at the areas set aside for keynote speakers, where we examined the diverse experiences which had led many artists to success. Our similar levels of ambition and determination helped artists to attain their career goals. We all see the value of learning from one another’s experiences and individual achievements in our field, as we share the same passions, motivations and objectives. Nobody knows it all - but during the conferences over a four day period we all agreed that there was a significant lack of knowledge throughout the industry from breaking musicians and their management teams. This, clearly needed to change.   
  
  
  
It was harrowing to see so many aspiring artists opposite me making the same mistakes so familiar to me in my earlier career. Fifteen years later, sitting on the other side of the panel, I felt it was my ethical duty to reveal my journey, and showing what can be learnt from my experiences,  helping the artists to create a successful story and future for themselves. I picked the mic up confidently, the audience in apprehensive silence. I introduced myself, thanked the organisers and other panelists and went ahead with our discussion. With sprightly vigour I explained to the audience “You can all become a success, if you do things in the right way” - a fellow panelist later called me a dreamcatcher with a wry smile, commending my insight. In my mind this is not dreaming - this is the reality. Anyone can achieve anything they want if they really have the passion, direction and determination for it.   
  
  
  
The band whom I was representing at the conference with Vicky Hamilton took me out to an open air bar in the extreme Texan humidity (there was a tropical rainstorm just before my arrival at this desert-based city) after their successful performance that night. . The band, heavily tattooed and sporting cowboy hats, followed by a swarm of groupies, stood out like a sore thumb - or did I? There was no air conditioning in what was called the ‘Dirt Bar’ - we were exposed to the raw, intolerable heat of Texas; I’ve travelled the world but never experienced such a humid climate before. The bass player repeated the same advice over and over throughout the evening - “You’ve gotta drink water, man, you’ve gotta drink water…” I didn’t think much of it having spent most of my time in comfortable air-conditioned facilities, but adhered to his directions skeptically. Later on, chatting with the frontman, he explained in a strong Californian accent that he had invested “...a million dollars in this band up until now,” I gathered some more information and knew that had he used that money more wisely in the past, it would have been a game-changer for the band. Seeing so much wasted only encouraged me to further my ethical approach, as I saw the determination and potential they had in their niche. However, the U.S music industry bled them dry and left them staggering, none the wiser.   
  
  
  
After speaking with the conference organisers, it emerged that the band was frowned upon due to their failure to use any of the educational resources provided by the event. They only performed their live showcase that I introduced, seemingly ignorant to the benefits our knowledge could have brought them. It is this attitude that lets artists down and is surely a factor which enabled the music industry to suck a million dollars out of them in previous years, preventing the proliferation of the band’s success.    
  
  
  
Later that night I woke up fearing my life was going to come to an abrupt end as a result of chronic dehydration. I rushed to the cabinet and quickly downed a litre of water. Now I understood why this enthusiastic musician had been so keen for me to guzzle that vital H2O.   
  
  
  
Massively jet-lagged and brought to the point where I felt like my life was endangered, I sat in the room alone… this was a moment I’d never forget. I sat on the plush linen and began to reminisce. It was not long ago that I was the artist on the other side of the fence. I recalled how it all began, and how my persistence has paid off - to become an expert in Public Relations / Branding in the entertainment world. Fifteen years of work throughout the music industry has honed the expertise needed to take me to where I am. It has not been an easy journey but I’m certainly glad that I persevered, continuing to do what I love and have a deep passion for.   
  
  
  
The Struggle IS Real! [Ch.2].  
  
  
  
I can recall a memory from my childhood, aged about four years old. I used to walk around the house trying to click my fingers. I couldn’t do it to begin with, but after persisting for three months on a daily basis I finally succeeded. Although this is a banal recollection, the lesson is a profound one, from which anybody, including you, can learn. My career in music is similar in essence - I started in my cousin’s shed, hand-writing reviews of promos so I could get free pre-released vinyls to use in my DJ sets on radio. I was fourteen. This set me on a long but focussed path, encountering valuable experiences and making important mistakes from which I would learn. I began to acquire the crucial skills which paved the road up to this point and beyond.   
  
  
  
I can remember a time when I was playing at dingy clubs for crowds of two hundred people, sleeping on a mate’s sofa afterwards.
