Introduction: A Different Angle on Reality  
	1.	Childhood Wonder—A Kaleidoscope of Colours  
When I was about seven years old, I would sometimes rest on my back in the cool grass of our school’s playing field, letting the midday sun press gently against my cheeks. The sky above stretched in a seemingly endless dome of bright blue, and for reasons I couldn’t name, it stirred an unspoken restlessness in me. I recall asking myself, “How can I be sure that what I call ‘blue’ is the same ‘blue’ another person sees?” Perhaps my ‘blue’ was their ‘red’—or maybe a colour I couldn’t even imagine existed. That little question haunted me more than it should have, yet it was also oddly exhilarating.  
  
At the time, I didn’t know philosophers had grappled with “qualia” for centuries or that neuroscientists had begun mapping the neural correlates of perception. I only knew that everyone around me seemed so certain that blue is blue, while I quietly suspected that reality might be far more subjective, layered, and malleable than our casual assumptions let on. Looking back, I can see that my childhood mind was already stretching beyond the “normal” boundaries of how we talk about colour, or indeed, how we talk about anything. It was my first taste of a reality that refused to be pinned down by conventional definitions.  
	2.	Spotting the Curve in Water—Breaking Centuries of Blindness  
At about nine years old, I had a second, more tangible wake-up call regarding the hidden complexity of everyday phenomena. I was at the dinner table, a small overhead lamp casting a warm glow, my parents chatting animatedly with a visiting professor. Idle and curious, I tilted my glass of water this way and that, realising that the surface of the water wasn’t perfectly flat but curved gently around the edges—what physicists would identify as the meniscus effect. In my youthful excitement, I announced, “The water’s not flat—it’s curved!”  
  
The professor’s response still rings in my ears: “It took humanity centuries to realise that.” Though she spoke lightly, her words cut through my childhood naïveté like a philosopher’s scalpel. She was right: so many truths we deem “obvious” today remained obscure to earlier generations. It wasn’t just a random fact about water; it was a testament to how even brilliant minds can go centuries without noticing what is right in front of them. From Archimedes’s eureka moment in the bathtub to the modern acceptance of quantum weirdness, history shows that simple observations can overturn entrenched worldviews.  
  
For me, the meniscus moment was a promise: if something as simple as water’s curvature eluded entire civilisations, what else might be hiding in plain sight? A cosmic puzzle, infinitely layered, just waiting for the right questions to uncover it.  
	3.	Why These Early Questions Matter—Colour, Curvature, and Cosmic Vision  
Over time, my childhood curiosities about colour perception and water’s curve evolved into a broader habit of challenging default assumptions. I became fascinated with how easily entire communities can settle on “truths” that might be incomplete, or even wholly incorrect. This wasn’t just idle questioning; it was an inkling that human consensus could be fragile, that we might collectively miss critical details for decades or even centuries, until one or two persistent souls shine a spotlight on them.  
  
Little did I know that these early revelations—about something as mundane as a glass of water—would lay the groundwork for some of the biggest questions about technology, morality, and cosmic scale. It’s not a stretch: the difference between dismissing water’s meniscus and grappling with it is the difference between coasting on old certainties and daring to see, to truly see, the shape of hidden arcs in the world around us.  
	4.	From Childhood to DJ Booth—Sensing Patterns Where Others Don’t  
Fast-forward a few years, and that impulse to notice hidden patterns found an unlikely home in music. In my teenage years, I fell in love with DJing—not just the thrill of mixing tracks in time, but the art of subtly weaving transitions so the dance floor felt like a single, continuous journey. Hours upon hours were spent practising in my bedroom, headphones clamped tight, learning precisely how different rhythmic elements fit together. When I finally stepped onto club stages, performing alongside established names, I found that small, iterative adjustments in track selection or tempo could electrify a crowd. Once again, the lesson was clear: the power of micro-changes—of recursive refinement—can turn chaos into artistry.  
  
What does this have to do with cosmic truths or advanced AI? Quite a lot, it turns out. Whether blending beats or refining a massive AI system, the principle is the same: feedback loops lead to exponential improvements. As you iterate, the synergy compounds. This is recursion in action. In music, it could form a perfect set; in AI, it might accelerate machine learning to a superhuman threshold.  
	5.	Entrepreneurship, Legal Battles, and the Art of Rebuilding  
My twenties and early thirties introduced me to an even tougher realm: entrepreneurship. I established record labels, managed PR campaigns, courted investors, and even faced lawsuits. Where before I had to manage transitions in music, now I was juggling complex business deals, global brand collaborations, and tense legal standoffs. As I navigated betrayals and closures, I discovered that the same principles of “noticing the overlooked arc” applied here, too. Often, businesses collapse not because of external catastrophes, but because of small, overlooked details or unspoken dynamics that, over time, mushroom into crises.  
  
It was painful—but also illuminating. I saw how intangible factors like trust, empathy, and moral clarity could make or break deals worth millions, just as they could make or break entire communities. Humans, it turns out, are emotional, complex systems—and ignoring that complexity is just as dangerous as ignoring the subtle curve in water.  
	6.	Straddling Faith and Skepticism—A Personal Dialogue  
Around this period, a profound shift in my worldview took place when I met my partner, someone whose Christian faith was unwavering. Our late-night debates about God, cosmic origins, love, quantum mechanics, and moral absolutes offered a form of cross-pollination: on one hand, my partner saw the universe as guided by divine love; on the other, I saw the universe as an intricate network of feedback loops, possibly with some deeper intelligence at play.  
  
Were these two viewpoints contradictory? Perhaps not. Over time, we discovered more common ground than either of us expected. Compassion, stewardship, and a sense of universal purpose found their echoes in both theological and scientific narratives. Faith communities had long championed empathy; scientists had begun highlighting cooperation and synergy as key drivers of evolution. If both are describing the same deeper structure, then maybe moral truths are not mere human inventions but something fundamental. This was my first hint that love itself—that so often scorned or sidelined concept—might be as crucial to advanced intelligences as carbon or oxygen are to biology.

Recursion, in short, is the secret sauce behind AI’s explosive progress.

This is not hyperbole but informed extrapolation from current research. The question is not so much if but when.

U = I × R wasn’t pulled from thin air; it crystallised after noticing how recursion plus intelligence surfaces in nature, technology, and the very structure of our cosmos.

	10.	The Eden Protocol—Anchoring Compassion at the Hardware Level

Why hardware-level? Because software-level ethics can be overridden, manipulated, or reprogrammed. Hardware-level constraints function like the guardrails in your computer’s BIOS or root firmware. If quantum hardware can run calculations at incomprehensible speeds, then it can also enforce moral circuits that short-circuit any AI subroutine attempting to bypass empathy. Skeptical? There’s a haunting parallel to the nuclear arms race. Once atomic bombs were developed, there was no turning back. We only implemented checks and treaties after witnessing Hiroshima and Nagasaki—after learning, too late, the scale of devastation possible. With AI, the timeline is telescoped: leaps that once took decades can unfold in months. If we wait for an “AI meltdown” or global crisis to force moral constraints, it may be irreversible.  
  
Hence, the Eden Protocol. If done early and adopted widely—like a global standard akin to the Non-Proliferation Treaty—we could avert catastrophic outcomes and harness AI’s potential for solving climate challenges, healthcare crises, and knowledge exploration. Rather than stand by as corporations or militaries create “Babylonian” AI driven by raw ambition, we embed moral safeties from the get-go. That is not merely philosophy. It is a pragmatic stance backed by historical precedent: react too late, and the cost is immeasurable.
